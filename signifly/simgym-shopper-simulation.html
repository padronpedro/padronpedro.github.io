Priority 1 — Conversion blockers

Shoppers left without finding what they came for

These two findings cost completed journeys: shoppers had clear intent, searched repeatedly, and abandoned without reaching a product. They are the highest-return fixes.

1
Add a direct “Bags” collection page, or surface leather accessories more prominently in Shoes & Accessories
High
What shoppers did

Several shoppers specifically looking for premium leather bags or handbags filtered into Shoes & Accessories and found only the North Face collaboration duffels. They then searched “leather bag”, “handbag”, and “tote” — with no relevant results — and left without finding what they were looking for.

Recommendation

Create a dedicated Bags collection page, or ensure the Bags filter in Shoes & Accessories reliably surfaces every available bag product. If the bag assortment is genuinely limited to collaborations, make that clear on the collection page so shoppers aren’t sent on a fruitless search.

Why it matters

This is high-intent, high-margin demand walking out the door. A shopper searching three times for the same category has already decided to buy — they simply can’t find the product.

2
Make the Outerwear / Jackets path more discoverable from search results for “jacket” queries
High
What shoppers did

Shoppers searching for “jacket” or “jackets” hit results cluttered with unrelated items (shoes, bags) and category filters that were unresponsive. They eventually found jackets only by manually navigating Shop › Outerwear — several extra steps later.

Recommendation

When shoppers search “jacket”, surface the Outerwear collection prominently — via a collection banner at the top of results or a working category filter. A direct “Shop Outerwear” suggestion would save multiple navigation steps.

Why it matters

Search is a strong-intent surface. Unresponsive filters and noisy results erode trust at exactly the moment a shopper is closest to a purchase decision.

Priority 2 — Discoverability & merchandising

Good products that shoppers had to work too hard to find

The products exist and shoppers wanted them — but the path to them was indirect. These changes reduce friction and shorten journeys.

3
Surface the Knitwear collection link in the main homepage category navigation
Medium
What shoppers did

Several shoppers looking for knitwear went to Tops first, then discovered the Knitwear collection only via a footer link. Although Knitwear does appear in the header Category dropdown, shoppers did not find it there intuitively — the footer was the discovery path.

Recommendation

Add a visible Knitwear link to the homepage category row alongside Dresses, Tops, and Shoes & Accessories — matching the discoverability those categories already enjoy.

4
Add a material-based filter to the Tops collection to help shoppers find silk and natural-fibre pieces
Medium
What shoppers did

Several shoppers browsing tops searched “silk” or “wool” via site search after finding many tops used synthetic blends — and those searches did not return useful filtered results.

Recommendation

Add a material filter (e.g. Cotton, Silk, Cashmere, Recycled) to the Tops collection page, so shoppers can identify natural-fibre pieces without opening individual product pages.

Why it matters

Material is a primary buying criterion for the luxury customer. Making it filterable signals quality and respects how this audience actually shops.

5
Link the “Our Universe” and brand story pages from product pages to support in-context trust building
Medium
What shoppers did

Several shoppers — after reviewing product details — navigated to the About or Our Universe page to validate the brand’s heritage before committing to a purchase.

Recommendation

Add a subtle link or short brand-story snippet on product pages (e.g. a brand note near the price) pointing to “Our Universe” or the craftsmanship philosophy. This keeps shoppers close to the add-to-cart action instead of sending them on a detour.

Keep doing this

A pattern that’s actively helping shoppers

✓ The Recently Viewed section supports multi-product comparison journeys

Many shoppers used the Recently Viewed section on product pages to move between items they’d already seen, enabling side-by-side comparison of dresses, skirts, sandals, and tops without returning to the collection page.

This was especially valuable for luxury shoppers who viewed four to six products before forming a preference — it measurably reduced the number of navigation steps needed to revisit a product.

Recommendation: keep this feature, and consider extending the same comparison-friendly pattern to other touchpoints (e.g. collection pages or the cart).

Recommended next steps

  1. Align on the two high-priority fixes (Bags & Outerwear search) and scope them for the next sprint.
  2. Confirm assortment reality for bags — decide whether to build a Bags collection or to set expectations on the existing page.
  3. Audit search & collection filters for responsiveness, starting with the “jacket” and material queries called out above.
  4. Ship the low-effort wins (Knitwear homepage link, Our Universe link) alongside the larger work.
  5. Re-run the simulation after changes to confirm the friction points are resolved.
